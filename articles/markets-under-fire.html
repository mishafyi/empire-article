Markets Under Fire

84 years of S&P 500 data across 7 major U.S. military conflicts reveal Wall Street's most counterintuitive pattern — and one variable that could break it.

When America goes to war, Wall Street panics — then profits. Since Pearl Harbor, every completed U.S. military conflict has ended with the S&P 500 higher than the day the shooting started. The average maximum drawdown is −21.5%. The median return by war's end is +39%. Here's the data behind the most counterintuitive pattern in financial history.

01
The Reflex — Sell Everything

The pattern starts the same way every time. A geopolitical shock — an invasion, an attack, a crossing of a line drawn in the sand — triggers immediate selling. The Korean War's first trading day saw the S&P plunge 5.4%, the biggest single-day drop of its era. Iraq's invasion of Kuwait in August 1990 sent the index down 16% in two months. Pearl Harbor, the defining surprise attack of the twentieth century, triggered a 3.5% Dow decline on Day 1 — then a grinding four-month slide to −25%.

The severity varies, but the reflex is universal. Short, decisive conflicts produce sharp but shallow dips — the Gulf War's trough came in two months and the S&P fully recovered within six. Longer, murkier wars produce deeper drawdowns that compound with unrelated economic cycles — Afghanistan's worst point (−37% from war start) came from the 2008 financial crisis, not Taliban offensives.

The pattern holds regardless of war type, duration, or geopolitical context. Hot wars, cold-turned-hot wars, asymmetric wars, regime-change wars. Six out of six completed conflicts. The market sells the invasion, then buys the recovery.

02
The Mobilization — Why Wars Make Markets

Wars are fiscal stimulus programs that don't need congressional debate. The mechanism is direct: the government opens the spending taps, defense contracts multiply, factories hire, GDP surges. During World War II, U.S. manufacturing output doubled between 1941 and 1943. Unemployment fell from 14.6% in 1940 to 1.2% by 1944. The S&P composite rose 58% from Pearl Harbor to V-J Day — not despite the war, but because it created the greatest industrial mobilization in human history.

The Korean War defense buildup catalyzed the entire 1950s economic expansion. Vietnam-era spending — military and Lyndon Johnson's Great Society programs combined — drove GDP growth averaging over 4% annually through the mid-1960s. Even the Gulf War, which lasted only seven months, cleared the uncertainty overhang of an oil shock and launched the 1990s bull run.

For investors, the arithmetic is simple: wars redirect enormous capital flows through the economy. Defense stocks surge first, then industrials, then consumer discretionary as employment and wages rise. The market follows the money — and in wartime, the money flows fast.

The time to buy is when there's blood in the streets, even if the blood is your own.

04
The Iran Variable

On Saturday, February 28, 2026, U.S. and Israeli forces launched coordinated strikes against Iranian military infrastructure, reportedly killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. When markets opened Monday, the S&P 500 barely moved — rising 0.04%. Oil spiked 10–13% to $80–82 per barrel, but equities shrugged.

By mid-week, European and Asian markets showed more anxiety — the DAX fell 3.4%, the Nikkei dropped 3.1%. The S&P touched an intraday low around 6,724, a modest −2.3% from the pre-war close. By March 4, it had recovered to 6,870.

The muted initial reaction is textbook. Markets have absorbed 84 years of precedent: the first-day panic is usually overdone, and wars tend to be net positive for equities. But the Iran conflict carries a variable that no previous conflict posed: the Strait of Hormuz.

The Hormuz Risk — The Binary That Changes Everything
  • Oil flowing through the Strait ~17–18M bbl/day
  • Share of global traded oil ~20%
  • Current oil price (post-strike) $80–82/bbl
  • Estimated oil if Strait closes $150–200/bbl
  • GDP drag per $10/bbl increase −0.2–0.3%

Twenty percent of the world's traded oil passes through this 21-mile waterway between Iran and Oman. A closure would dwarf the 1973 OPEC embargo.

Previous wars boosted U.S. GDP through defense spending alone. An Iran war that closes the Strait would simultaneously boost defense spending and crater global energy markets. The net effect is uncertain — and that uncertainty is what makes Iran fundamentally different from the six conflicts that came before.

If the Strait stays open, the historical pattern predicts a buying opportunity. If it closes, 84 years of precedent may not survive the collision with $200 oil.

05
The Bottom Line

Six for six. Every completed major U.S. military conflict since 1941 has ended with the S&P higher — often dramatically — than when the war began. The median return is +39%. The average maximum drawdown is −21.5%. The pattern isn't luck; it reflects a structural reality: war mobilization is economic stimulus at massive scale.

The seventh war is still being written. The S&P's initial reaction to Iran follows the historical script: contained, modest, quickly absorbed. But the Strait of Hormuz remains the single variable that could break 84 years of precedent.

Eighty-four years of data deserves respect. But so does the 21-mile waterway that carries a fifth of the world's oil. ■

The Gravity of Power

Why power always pools, why empires always die, and why the material always reforms somewhere else.

PrologueWhy stars?

Artificial intelligence might be the most concentrating technology ever built. Or the most distributing. We don't know yet. The companies building frontier models are worth more than most countries. The models themselves could, in theory, make centuries of accumulated institutional advantage replicable overnight. Which outcome we get may be the most consequential question of the next fifty years.

But to answer it, you have to understand something older. For about 5,000 years, power has pooled in certain places and radiated outward, pulling resources and labor back toward the center. The location changes. The technology changes. The justifying story changes. The pattern doesn't.

This essay argues that the pattern works like stellar physics. Not as a metaphor. As a model.

A random patch of dust in space ends up slightly denser than its surroundings. That small advantage pulls in more material. More mass, more pull, more mass. A feedback loop that turns a slight imbalance into a star. Once the star forms, it holds — fusion pushing outward against gravity pulling inward — for as long as the fuel lasts. When the fuel runs out, the star collapses. But the material doesn't vanish. A supernova's shockwave compresses nearby gas clouds and triggers new star formation. The death of one center causes the birth of the next.

This maps precisely onto what we see across five millennia of human civilization. Mesopotamia had fertile river valleys. Baghdad sat on trade crossroads. Britain had coal in the right place. Each was a patch of dust slightly denser than its surroundings. Each compounded a small advantage into a center of gravity. Each held through equilibrium — output balancing cost — until the fuel ran out. And each collapse scattered material from which the next center formed.

In the 1970s, the sociologist Immanuel Wallerstein formalized this as world-systems theory: at any point in history, the world sorts itself into a core that sets rules and captures surplus, a periphery that supplies raw materials and labor, and a semi-periphery in between. Other scholars — Andre Gunder Frank, Barry Gills — argued the pattern goes back not to Wallerstein's starting point of 1500 but to the Bronze Age. When you trace it, they're hard to argue with.

Others have circled pieces of this idea. Marx described capital as subject to "attraction" and "repulsion" — gravitational language borrowed from Newton. Simone Weil, in Gravity and Grace, called gravity the force that "impels each creature to seek everything which can preserve or enlarge it." Henry Adams applied thermodynamics to history and predicted accelerating crisis in 1909. Spengler used biology. Toynbee used challenge-and-response. Ibn Khaldun used social cohesion.

Nobody mapped the full stellar lifecycle — accretion, equilibrium, collapse, shockwave, reformation — onto the rotation of world-system cores across five thousand years. That's what this essay does. Four phases, then a question: does AI introduce a fifth — physics that's never existed before?

Phase IAccretion — why centers form

In astrophysics, star formation begins with a molecular cloud — a vast field of gas and dust, mostly uniform. But not perfectly uniform. Some region, by chance or by external compression, ends up slightly denser than the rest. That density advantage creates a gravitational pull. The pull draws in more material. More material increases the mass. More mass strengthens the pull. A feedback loop that turns a slight imbalance into a star.

The same loop has driven every center of power in recorded history.

Mesopotamia: the original density advantage

The earliest version we can identify. The Tigris and Euphrates gave southern Mesopotamia something nowhere else had in the same combination: fertile soil, reliable water, and a flat floodplain where irrigation could scale. That was the initial density advantage — the patch of dust slightly denser than the rest.

Irrigation produced grain surplus. Surplus meant not everyone had to farm, which freed labor for specialization — potters, metalworkers, scribes, soldiers. Managing that complexity required administration, which produced writing. Protecting it required armies. Extending it required trade networks. Each step funded the next. Surplus created infrastructure. Infrastructure attracted people. People produced more surplus. More mass, more pull, more mass.

Within a few centuries, these river valley civilizations — Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, Shang China — had become the first cores. They stockpiled grain, developed writing systems, built armies, and projected power outward. The Hittites in Anatolia and the Minoans on Crete sat in between, middlemen who processed and traded and occasionally challenged the established centers. The periphery supplied what the centers couldn't produce themselves.

Even 5,000 years ago, the geometry was recognizable: core, semi-periphery, periphery. Resources and labor flowing inward. Influence and rules radiating outward.

The technology that defined the era — bronze — was also what revealed the system's structure. Tin and copper almost never occur in the same place. Mesopotamia needed tin from Afghanistan and copper from Cyprus. The center depended on a supply chain that stretched thousands of miles. The feedback loop that built the center also built the dependencies that would eventually destroy it.

Britain: the same physics, industrial inputs

Fast-forward four thousand years. Britain in the mid-1700s had a density advantage that looked nothing like Mesopotamia's but operated identically. Coal deposits — massive ones, in the right places, near navigable rivers and ports. An island geography that made naval defense cheaper than land defense. A financial system, developed through centuries of trade, that could fund large-scale enterprise.

The feedback loop: coal powered steam engines. Steam engines pumped water out of coal mines, letting them go deeper, extracting more coal. More coal powered more machines. Machines multiplied output by orders of magnitude. That output funded more machines, more infrastructure, more naval power, more colonial extraction. Within two generations, one country's coal advantage had compounded into control of global trade, the reserve currency, and the world's largest empire.

Nobody planned this. Britain didn't sit down and decide to become the center of the world. It had a slightly denser patch of resources and institutions, and a mechanism — industrialization — that compounded the advantage faster than anything before it.

Silicon Valley: modern accretion

Stanford University plus military R&D contracts in the 1950s. That was the initial density. Fairchild Semiconductor spun out of Shockley's lab. Fairchild's alumni founded Intel, AMD, and dozens of others. Venture capital emerged to fund the spinoffs. The spinoffs attracted more talent. The talent attracted more capital. The capital funded more companies.

The same feedback loop: a slight initial density advantage, compounding through a mechanism that fed itself. Now a narrow strip of Northern California controls the technology stack that the rest of the world runs on.

None of these centers were destined. Mesopotamia wasn't chosen. Britain wasn't inevitable. Silicon Valley was an accident of Cold War defense spending and one physicist's bad management. They each had a slight initial advantage and a mechanism that compounded it. The pattern is structural, not cultural or moral. Any patch of dust dense enough will become a star.

Phase IIEquilibrium — why centers hold

Once a star forms, it doesn't just keep collapsing. Fusion ignites in the core — hydrogen atoms fusing into helium, releasing enormous energy. That energy pushes outward as radiation pressure. Gravity pulls inward. As long as the fuel lasts, neither force wins. That tension is the stable state. A star can burn for billions of years in equilibrium. It looks permanent. It isn't. It's a balanced war.

Every center of power in history has run on its own version of this tension.

Rome: centuries of balanced war

Rome is the clearest case — a center that held equilibrium for longer than most, and whose mechanics are well enough documented to see the physics clearly.

The outward force — Rome's fusion — was institutional. Legal frameworks that standardized commerce across three continents. A common currency that let a merchant in Gaul trade with one in Syria. Roads that moved armies and goods at speeds that wouldn't be matched for over a thousand years. Aqueducts that made cities viable in places where they shouldn't have been. Grain from Egypt, metals from Iberia, luxury goods from the East — all flowing inward through a system that worked because the rules were predictable and the infrastructure was real.

The inward pull — gravity — was cost. Defending borders that stretched from Britain to Mesopotamia. Feeding an army of over 300,000. Maintaining roads, ports, aqueducts, and administrative systems across a territory that took months to cross. Managing the political complexity of dozens of provinces, each with its own elites demanding a cut.

Rome lasted centuries not because it was invincible but because the energy it generated — taxes, trade revenue, institutional legitimacy — kept pace with the cost of its own weight. The whole Mediterranean operated as one economic zone. That was the equilibrium. Not peace. Tension.

The signs of stable equilibrium are always the same: everyone uses your currency, your roads, your legal system. The center becomes the default. The cost of opting out exceeds the cost of opting in. That's not domination in the traditional sense. It's gravity.

The Han Dynasty: parallel equilibrium

On the other side of the world, the Han Dynasty ran an almost identical balancing act. The Silk Road projected Chinese goods and influence outward. Central Asian oasis cities served as semi-periphery — middlemen connecting the Han core to markets further west. Steppe nomads at the edges functioned as periphery, periodically raiding, occasionally reshaping everything.

The Han's fusion was similar to Rome's: agricultural surplus from the Yellow and Yangtze river basins, a sophisticated bureaucracy selected by examination, standardized weights and measures, a legal system that held the territory together. The gravity was similar too: cost of defending the northern frontier, managing regional elites, maintaining the infrastructure that made the system work.

These two equilibria — Roman and Han — ran in parallel, loosely linked through the Silk Road and Indian Ocean trade routes. A proto-global economy, thousands of years before the word "globalization" existed.

The United States: institutional equilibrium

After World War II, the United States built the most sophisticated version of the pattern. Instead of territorial control, it created institutions — the UN, IMF, World Bank, NATO, WTO — and made the dollar the operating currency of global trade.

The outward force: the Bretton Woods system, then the petrodollar arrangement, meant international commerce flowed through American currency. GPS, the internet, semiconductor supply chains — the technological infrastructure that the world runs on was designed in and controlled from the American core. The alliance network provided security guarantees that made the American system the default.

The inward pull: maintaining 750+ military bases in 80+ countries. Funding the institutions. Managing the political complexity of a global alliance network. The cost of being the center.

Same physics. More sophisticated mechanism. The question, as always, is how long the fuel lasts.

The moment the cost of holding everything together exceeds what the system produces, equilibrium breaks. It doesn't happen suddenly. The imbalance starts small and compounds — the same feedback loop that built the center, running in reverse.

Phase IIICollapse — why centers die

Fuel runs out. Fusion stops. Gravity wins. But not all stars die the same way.

Small stars cool slowly into white dwarfs. The structure persists, the light fades, and the process takes a very long time. Massive stars explode in supernovae — fast, violent, total. The core collapses in seconds and the shockwave tears the outer layers apart.

The type of death determines what comes next. Empires die differently too, and the distinction matters for exactly the same reason.

Supernova: the Bronze Age collapse

Around 1200 BC, the ancient world experienced what may be the purest systemic supernova on record. Nearly every major civilization collapsed within a few decades: Mycenaean Greece, the Hittite Empire, the Egyptian New Kingdom, Ugarit, the Kassite dynasty in Babylon. A cascade so thorough that written records nearly disappeared for centuries afterward.

The Bronze Age system had been in equilibrium for roughly two thousand years. The fuel was agricultural surplus and long-distance trade. The technology that defined the era — bronze — was also its deepest vulnerability. Tin and copper almost never occur in the same place. Mesopotamia needed tin from Afghanistan and copper from Cyprus. The Hittites depended on trade routes that stretched across Anatolia. The Mycenaeans imported copper from Cyprus and tin from as far as Cornwall. The entire system was one supply chain.

Then several things broke at once. Prolonged drought cut the agricultural surplus that these societies ran on — the fuel. The "Sea Peoples," probably displaced populations from the collapsing edges, raided coastal cities, disrupting the trade routes. Tin and copper stopped flowing. Without bronze, the military and economic machinery seized up. Internal revolts followed, as populations that could no longer be fed or controlled turned against their own centers.

No single problem would have been fatal. Any one of these shocks, in isolation, might have been absorbed. But the system was so interconnected that when several links broke at once, the cascade was total. First systemic crisis on record. Interconnection and fragility turned out to be the same thing.

The collapse was fast. Within a generation, the political and economic structures that had held for millennia were gone. Palace economies that had centralized writing, administration, and trade simply ceased to exist. The Greek world entered a Dark Age that lasted centuries. That's a supernova — not a gradual decline but a violent detonation that scatters everything.

White dwarf: Rome's slow cooling

Rome died differently. The equilibrium didn't shatter. It eroded.

The silver content of the denarius — Rome's standard coin — dropped from 95% under Augustus to under 5% by the late third century. That's not a policy choice. That's a system running out of fuel and debasing the currency to buy time. Borders that had been defensible when the army was well-funded became porous as resources thinned. Regional commanders became warlords when the center could no longer pay or control them. Administrative complexity that had been a strength became a cost the system couldn't bear.

The periphery didn't conquer Rome. Rome lost the ability to hold itself together. The energy it generated no longer covered the cost of its own weight. The light faded. The structure held — nominally — for decades after the substance was gone. A white dwarf.

The Han Dynasty fell to a similar combination half a world away: court infighting, regional warlords, steppe pressure on the frontier. Parallel collapses on opposite sides of the world, driven by the same physics. When equilibrium breaks, it doesn't matter what you call the empire.

The star that never ignited: Ming China

Between 1405 and 1433, Admiral Zheng He led seven massive expeditions across the Indian Ocean. His fleet included ships over 400 feet long — the largest wooden vessels ever built. Columbus's Santa Maria, sixty years later, was 62 feet. China had the naval capability, the institutional sophistication, the manufacturing base, and the population to become the dominant global center before Europe ever reached open water.

Then the Ming court turned inward. The Haijin edicts restricted maritime trade. The treasure fleet was dismantled. Confucian bureaucrats who saw commerce as beneath the state won the argument over admirals who saw opportunity beyond the horizon. Within a generation, the most advanced navy in the world was firewood.

This is the single strongest case in 5,000 years of a potential core choosing not to occupy the center. The mass was there. The density advantage was there. The feedback loop was primed. They chose not to ignite.

And the seat didn't stay empty. Portugal and Spain filled it within decades. The structure doesn't care about the intentions of any particular player. When one center withdraws, another forms. The game doesn't need you. It only needs someone.

Phase IVShockwave — how death causes rebirth

Here is the part that nobody has written about.

In astrophysics, a supernova doesn't just destroy a star. The shockwave — expanding outward at tens of thousands of kilometers per second — compresses nearby gas clouds. That compression triggers new gravitational collapse in clouds that were previously too diffuse to form stars on their own. The death of one star causes the formation of the next. Our own solar system likely formed from material compressed by a nearby supernova roughly 4.6 billion years ago.

The same causal link runs through every major transition in human civilization. Centers don't just die. Their deaths create the conditions — displaced knowledge, freed trade routes, power vacuums, scattered populations — from which the next center forms.

Rome's debris becomes the Islamic Golden Age

When Rome's light faded, the material it had accumulated didn't vanish. Greek philosophical texts, Persian administrative knowledge, Indian mathematical innovations, Egyptian engineering — all of it was scattered across the former Roman and Sassanid peripheries. The knowledge was there. It needed a new center of gravity to collect it.

The Islamic caliphates were that center. Sitting on every major trade route between three continents — geographic density advantage — they pulled in the scattered material and fused it into something new. The House of Wisdom in Baghdad systematically translated and built on Greek, Persian, Indian, and Chinese knowledge. Algebra and the algorithm — both Arabic words — were synthesized there. Optics, early chemistry, astronomical tables that European navigators would rely on centuries later. Paper manufacturing, adopted from Chinese prisoners of war after the Battle of Talas in 751, let them scale the production of books in a way that Europe, still copying manuscripts on animal skin, couldn't match.

Baghdad had bookshops when London had mud streets.

The economy reflected this. The caliphates ran a unified monetary system across a territory larger than Rome's. A merchant could travel from Cordoba to Samarkand using the same currency and legal framework. Agricultural innovation — new irrigation systems, the introduction of rice, cotton, and sugarcane across the Mediterranean — drove the surplus that funded everything else. Per capita income in the Islamic world during this period likely exceeded anything in Europe until the late Renaissance.

Europe, meanwhile, was the periphery. Medieval Europeans exported timber, furs, and slaves — the word "slave" comes from "Slav" — to the Islamic world and Byzantium in exchange for finished goods. The trade imbalance was so persistent that European silver drained steadily eastward for centuries. Europe spent centuries in the periphery before its later rise. The current hierarchy isn't ancient or natural. It's recent.

This wasn't coincidence. It was shockwave physics. Rome's collapse scattered the material. The caliphates' geographic density advantage compressed it into a new center. The death of one directly caused the formation of the next.

Bronze Age debris becomes classical Greece

The Bronze Age supernova scattered populations, freed trade routes from palace-economy control, and broke the monopoly on literacy and administration that centralized states had maintained. The world that emerged from the Dark Age was radically different from the one that had collapsed.

Instead of centralized palace economies, the eastern Mediterranean produced distributed city-states — Athens, Corinth, Sparta — and the Phoenician trading networks that connected them. The Phoenicians, themselves products of the collapse (their cities like Tyre and Sidon survived where larger empires hadn't), developed the alphabet that would become the basis for Greek, Latin, Arabic, and eventually most of the world's writing systems.

The shockwave didn't recreate what was destroyed. It compressed new material into new forms. The supernova's violence was what made the transformation possible — a slower collapse might have produced a diminished version of the old system rather than something genuinely new.

Islamic fragmentation becomes European expansion

The Mongol destruction of Baghdad in 1258 was catastrophic — libraries burned, irrigation systems destroyed, the caliphate's center gutted. The Crusades disrupted Mediterranean trade routes. The fall of Constantinople in 1453 closed the overland routes to Asia that Europeans had depended on for luxury goods.

That scattering of material from the Islamic center — combined with the pressure to find new routes — became the fuel for European expansion. Portuguese sailors pushed down the African coast. Columbus went west. The joint-stock company, invented by the Dutch, made colonial ventures scalable. Double-entry bookkeeping made them trackable. The triangular trade — manufactured goods to Africa, enslaved labor to the Americas, raw materials back to Europe — extracted surplus from entire continents that hadn't previously been part of the world-system.

The Mongol Empire had played a crucial intermediate role. A periphery that overran the cores, it welded the separate world-systems into something closer to a single global one. China, Central Asia, Persia, Eastern Europe under one political roof. The Pax Mongolica moved goods and ideas at a scale nobody had seen before. It also moved the Black Death from Central Asia to Europe, killing roughly a third of the continent. Interconnection and fragility, traveling together again.

Britain's eventual rise to the center — coal, steam, naval dominance — was the end product of a chain that started with Rome's collapse, ran through the Islamic Golden Age, was scrambled by the Mongols, and was finally compressed into a new center by the specific combination of resources, institutions, and accidents that Britain happened to have.

Phase V?The new physics

The pattern has survived every previous technological revolution.

Agriculture. Bronze. Iron. Gunpowder. Printing. Steam. Electricity. Nuclear fission. The internet. Each one reshuffled who sits at the center. None changed the structure. New tools went to whoever already had power, and the geometry reasserted itself. Every technology that could have been decentralizing got absorbed by existing power structures instead.

The internet was going to democratize information. It produced a handful of companies worth more than most countries. Nuclear energy was going to be limitless. It became a geopolitical bargaining chip. Radio was going to educate the masses. It became a propaganda tool.

In the language of stellar physics, every previous technology was fuel. It powered the star — sometimes more efficiently, sometimes triggering a transition to a new center — but it didn't change the fundamental forces. Steam was fuel. Electricity was fuel. Nuclear was fuel. The internet was fuel. The center burned hotter, but it was still a star, and gravity still worked the same way.

AI might be different. Not because it's more powerful fuel. Because it might change gravity itself.

The core-periphery structure has always depended on information asymmetry. Centers know more. They have better institutions, deeper talent pools, more accumulated expertise. Every previous technology still needed massive human capital to deploy. You could ship a steam engine to a country that didn't have trained engineers, functioning institutions, and decades of industrial development — and it would sit there. The knowledge gap between center and periphery was structural, and every technology required that structural advantage to operate.

AGI changes that equation. If a system can reason at or beyond human level, the knowledge gap between center and periphery stops being structural. Centuries of accumulated institutional advantage become replicable. A country that never built a pharmaceutical industry could, in theory, design drugs. A region without a legal tradition could generate contracts. The thing that actually separates the tiers — differential access to expertise — could become universally available for the first time in recorded history.

That's not new fuel. That's a change in the gravitational constant. If intelligence becomes universally available, the force that creates centers weakens. Not a new rotation. A new physics.

But there's a reason 5,000 years of precedent should give us pause.

AI development right now is concentrated in a few organizations in a few countries. Training a frontier model costs hundreds of millions of dollars. The compute required is manufactured in a handful of fabrication plants, mostly in Taiwan, using equipment made by one Dutch company. If that concentration holds, AI doesn't flatten the hierarchy. It steepens it. The center gets superintelligent automation. The periphery gets chatbots. And AGI under centralized control isn't a structural shift — it's the most powerful concentration of capability ever built. Whoever runs it doesn't just sit at the center. They are the center, in a way that makes every previous hegemon look distributed by comparison.

If the pattern accelerates, the question isn't just who ends up at the center. It's whether the rotation speed exceeds the system's ability to stabilize. A star that fuses fuel faster and faster doesn't reach a new equilibrium. It detonates.

So whether AI breaks the 5,000-year pattern or writes its next chapter probably comes down to infrastructure. If models become open and capable on cheap hardware, there's a real window — maybe the first real window in recorded history — for the structure to change, not just the occupants.

But 5,000 years of precedent says new tools flow toward existing power centers before they flow anywhere else.

Stars form because the physics demands it. A slight density advantage, a feedback loop, and gravity does the rest. They hold because fuel balances collapse. They die because the fuel runs out. And their deaths seed the next generation — material scattering outward, compressing new clouds, triggering new collapses into new centers.

For five thousand years, power has followed the same physics. The material has never stayed scattered for long. The Bronze Age collapse took three centuries to produce classical Greece. Rome's slow cooling took two centuries to produce the Islamic Golden Age. But the material always reformed. There has always been a center.

The question — the only new question — is whether a technology that can replicate the thing that makes centers centers might finally change the physics that guarantees them. Not a new rotation. Not a new star forming from old debris. Something that has never happened in five millennia of recorded history.

Or maybe just the next star, burning hotter than the last, in the same old sky.

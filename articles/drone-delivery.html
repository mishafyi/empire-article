Death From Above: How Drones Will Bury DoorDash & Uber Eats

An $80 billion empire built on subsidized losses and 30% commissions is about to meet a $1-per-delivery competitor that doesn't need roads, drivers, or tips.

March 20269 min read

The food delivery duopoly of DoorDash and Uber Eats was built on a simple bet: spend billions subsidizing deliveries, capture the market, then raise prices later. There's just one problem — "later" arrived, and so did drones.

01
The Broken Model: A House Built on Burning Cash

DoorDash dominates the U.S. food delivery market with an estimated 55–67% share, depending on how the category is measured. Uber Eats holds about 23%. Together, they process tens of billions of dollars in orders annually. And for most of their existence, they've done it while losing staggering amounts of money.

DoorDash went public in December 2020 at a $72 billion valuation — despite never having posted an annual profit. The company had one profitable quarter in its history: Q2 2020, when COVID lockdowns created a captive market, netting a modest $23 million. It then proceeded to lose $1.4 billion in 2022 and $558 million in 2023. It scraped together its first annual profit in 2024: $123 million on $10.72 billion in revenue. In 2025, revenue climbed to $13.72 billion with $935 million in net income — a healthier 6.8% margin, but still modest for a company valued at $70–97 billion. Even after two profitable years, DoorDash's cumulative net loss since its IPO remains approximately −$1.8 billion.

How did it build that dominance? By selling dollars for ninety cents. In Q1 2019, DoorDash spent 101% of its revenue on sales and marketing — spending more on customer acquisition than it earned. Before its IPO, it had raised over $2.5 billion in venture capital. The IPO added another $3.4 billion. The Wolt acquisition cost $8 billion. The Deliveroo acquisition in late 2025 added another ~$3.9 billion. That's roughly $18 billion deployed to build a business that only turned profitable in 2024.

A more bearish model (WallStreetZen) estimates fair value at $104, suggesting the stock is overvalued by 118%. In February 2026, DoorDash missed Q4 2025 earnings ($0.48 EPS vs. $0.58 expected) and issued 2026 profit guidance far below consensus — shares initially plunged ~10%.

Uber Eats, while sheltered within Uber's larger profitable ride-hailing operation, has its own struggles. The delivery segment has historically never been independently profitable, and Uber as a whole recorded a $9.1 billion net loss in 2022 (partly due to investment write-downs). The entire food delivery app model has been, in the words of content strategist Ranjan Roy, "subsidized into market dominance."

Third-party delivery platforms, as they've been built, just seem like the wrong model, but instead of testing, failing, and evolving, they've been subsidized into market dominance.

02
Everyone Is Unhappy — The Three-Sided Squeeze

Restaurants: Working for Free

Third-party delivery platforms charge restaurants commissions ranging from 15% to 30% per order. DoorDash's tiers run 15% (Basic), 25% (Plus), and 30% (Premier). Uber Eats tops out at 30% on its Premium plan. Most restaurants end up on the higher tiers because that's where the visibility and DashPass eligibility live — the features they need to get orders in the first place.

Here's the brutal arithmetic: the average restaurant operates on a 10–15% profit margin. When a platform takes 30% off the top, the restaurant is losing money on every delivery order. On a $50 order at 30% commission, the platform takes $15. After food costs, labor, and overhead, there's nothing left.

And that 30% is just the headline number. Industry analysis reveals the true cost can exceed 40% of revenue when you factor in payment processing fees (2.9–3.5%), mandatory marketing promotions, and the menu price inflation restaurants are forced into. Platforms also control the customer data — research shows 43% of customers can't even recall which restaurant they ordered from after using a delivery app.

Commission Reality Check — $50 Order
  • Platform commission (30%) −$15.00
  • Payment processing (~3%) −$1.50
  • Food cost (~30%) −$15.00
  • Labor + overhead (~25%) −$12.50
  • Restaurant profit −$6.00

Consumers: Fee Fatigue

Consumers aren't faring much better. A New York Times analysis found that ordering through delivery apps can be 25% to 91% more expensive than ordering directly. A $25 meal can easily become $40+ after service fees, delivery fees, inflated menu prices, and tips.

According to Technomic, 41% of consumers who've reduced their delivery ordering cite high delivery fees as the reason. Another 48% point to inflated menu prices. Paytronix found that 63% of restaurant customers haven't used a third-party delivery service since 2020 — four in ten specifically blaming delivery fees. Weekend dinner hours routinely trigger fee spikes of 20–25% in urban areas.

The premium restaurants charge for delivery menus has nearly doubled since 2020, according to Gordon Haskett Research Advisors. And now states are piling on "doorstep taxes" — Minnesota charges 50 cents per delivery, Colorado adds 28 cents, and a dozen more states are evaluating similar fees.

Enter the Disruptors

What if delivery cost $1 instead of $8? What if it took 10 minutes instead of 40? What if you didn't need a driver, a car, or a tip?

03
The Drones Are Here — And They're Scaling Fast

This isn't a whitepaper fantasy. Drone delivery is operational, scaling, and accelerating in 2026. Three companies are leading the charge — each with distinct strategies, but all converging on the same conclusion: the gig-driver model is structurally obsolete.

Zipline — backed by Sequoia Capital, a16z, Fidelity, Baillie Gifford, Tiger Global, Valor Equity Partners, GV, and Temasek — raised $600 million in January 2026 at a $7.6 billion valuation. The company has raised over $1.2 billion in total. Founded in 2014 delivering blood via drone in Rwanda, Zipline has surpassed 2 million deliveries and 125 million autonomous commercial miles, delivering more than 20 million items without a single serious injury.

What separates Zipline is its Level 4 autonomous operation. Its drones fly without a pilot and without visual observers on the ground — the only human involvement is staff loading orders and remote operators monitoring flights from centralized operations centers. The FAA granted Zipline what the industry calls the "holy grail" approval: BVLOS operations without ground observers, enabling dramatically expanded service areas. Most flights are now fully autonomous. New deployment sites reach 100 deliveries per day in just 2 days — compared to 10 weeks for Zipline's first location. U.S. deliveries are growing roughly 15% week-over-week for seven consecutive months.

In the next five to ten years, deliveries made by autonomous aircraft will become standard. That revolution is going to be led by Zipline.

Antonio Gracias, CEO of Valor Equity Partners

Wing, Alphabet's drone subsidiary, is executing the world's largest drone delivery expansion with Walmart. By end of 2026, some 40 million Americans — roughly 12% of the U.S. population — will have access to drone delivery from Walmart stores. Wing is expanding to 150 additional stores this year (reaching 270+ by 2027) across Los Angeles, Miami, Houston, St. Louis, Cincinnati, Charlotte, Orlando, and Tampa. The top quartile of Wing customers order three times per week. Delivery volume has tripled in six months.

Flytrex is one of only four companies with FAA Beyond Visual Line of Sight authorization. It's targeting approximately $1 per delivery at operational cost, while charging consumers $0 delivery fees on most restaurant orders. Price comparisons show a Jimmy John's meal costing $12.99 through Flytrex versus $19.35 through Uber Eats — nearly 50% cheaper. Flytrex has partnered with both Uber Eats (which invested "tens of millions" in September 2025) and DoorDash, and plans to expand to 37 U.S. metro areas by 2026.

The cost asymmetry is the whole story. Sending a gig worker in a car costs platforms $6–$8 per delivery. Flytrex targets approximately $1 per delivery at scale. That's a 6 to 8x structural cost advantage. And unlike human drivers, that cost curve bends down over time as drone technology improves, battery costs fall, and regulatory approvals expand service areas.

04
The Innovator's Dilemma — Why the Giants Can't Fight Back

Clayton Christensen's framework has never fit a real-world situation this cleanly. DoorDash and Uber Eats are textbook incumbents facing a disruptive technology — trapped by their own success.

They can't lower prices. Their entire cost structure is built around paying human drivers. DoorDash has over 9 million couriers who earned $20 billion in 2025. You cannot restructure that cost base to compete with autonomous drones.

They can't cut commissions. At 15–30% restaurant commissions, their profitability is recent and fragile. DoorDash only turned its first annual profit in 2024 ($123M), and while 2025 improved to $935 million on $13.72 billion in revenue, that's still a 6.8% margin on a company valued at $70–97 billion — and the cumulative P&L since IPO is still −$1.8 billion in the red. If they match drone-operator commission rates, those margins compress fast.

They can't abandon their investments. Billions have been sunk into driver networks, DashPass/Wolt+/Deliveroo Plus subscriptions (35 million members) and Uber One (46 million members), marketing infrastructure, and two massive acquisitions — Wolt ($8B in stock dilution) and Deliveroo ($3.9B funded by convertible debt due 2030). These are irreversible commitments to a car-based delivery model.

Their investors won't let them. At a ~$80 billion market cap, DoorDash's shareholders expect growth and profitability — not a pivot to autonomous delivery that cannibalizes existing revenue. The stock already dropped ~10% on one earnings miss.

It is easy to sell a dollar for 90 cents and call yourself a growing business. However, achieving growth while also turning a profit is a tougher task.

The Motley Fool, on DoorDash's growth model

The Ironic Hedge

DoorDash isn't ignoring drones. Through its DoorDash Labs automation arm, the company has active drone partnerships with both major operators. With Wing, DoorDash launched drone delivery in Christiansburg, Virginia in 2024, expanded to Dallas-Fort Worth and Charlotte in 2025, and now delivers from 50+ restaurants across multiple malls and suburbs. With Flytrex, DoorDash launched service in Little Elm and Frisco, Texas — Flytrex's first third-party app integration — reaching over 30,000 households. Customers see a drone delivery option at checkout. Orders arrive in as little as 15 minutes. No tip required.

On the surface, this looks like smart hedging — DoorDash positions itself as the aggregator layer, routing orders to whichever mode is optimal. But look closer and the dilemma sharpens:

DoorDash doesn't own the drones. Wing and Flytrex control the hardware, the airspace authorization, the autonomous software, and the cost advantage. DoorDash is the app — a marketplace layer sitting on top of infrastructure it doesn't control. Every successful drone delivery proves the technology works, and once it's proven, the question becomes: why does a restaurant need to pay DoorDash's 30% commission when it can contract directly with a drone operator at ~$1 per delivery?

DoorDash is currently waiving drone delivery fees — subsidizing adoption of the very technology that threatens to disintermediate it. That playbook should sound familiar. It's the same VC-funded subsidy model that built DoorDash in the first place, except this time, DoorDash is subsidizing someone else's disruption.

Drone delivery eliminates tipping — which removes one of the few remaining friction points that keeps consumers loyal to a single platform. When delivery costs converge toward zero and tips disappear, the switching cost between apps approaches zero too.

DoorDash's drone bet is a hedge that accelerates the very disruption it's hedging against. The value capture shifts to the operators who own the delivery infrastructure. DoorDash goes from being the indispensable delivery backbone to being an optional software layer. That's a very different business — and a much less valuable one.

05
The Regulatory Tailwind

If cost economics are the engine, regulation is the accelerator. The FAA's proposed Part 108 rules, expected by spring 2026, will standardize BVLOS approvals, eliminating the need for individual waivers at each new location. Through October 2024, the FAA had issued only 190 total BVLOS waivers. Part 108 removes that bottleneck entirely.

The August 2025 FAA authorization allowing multiple drone companies to operate in the same airspace over Dallas-Fort Worth was a watershed moment. The Trump administration has moved to accelerate drone industry growth, issuing executive orders directing regulators to fast-track rules while prioritizing U.S.-made technology. The expansion math changes from "city by city, waiver by waiver" to "nationwide, on demand."

06
The Bottom Line

The food delivery app model was built on a fundamentally expensive infrastructure — human drivers in personal cars, coordinated by algorithms, paid by gig workers accepting unpredictable wages. That model required billions in subsidized growth, charged restaurants commissions so high they lose money on every order, hit consumers with fees that can double their food costs, and still couldn't consistently turn a profit.

Drones aren't a speculative future. Zipline — backed by Sequoia, a16z, and Fidelity — has completed over 2 million fully autonomous deliveries across 125 million miles without a serious injury. Wing is delivering from hundreds of Walmart stores to 40 million Americans. Flytrex is delivering food for $0 delivery fees. The FAA is about to unlock nationwide autonomous operations. The cost advantage is 6–8x.

DoorDash and Uber Eats are trapped. They can't lower prices because their cost structure won't allow it. They can't cut commissions because they'll compress their still-young profitability. They've partnered with drone operators — but in doing so, they're subsidizing the adoption of technology that makes their 30% take rate indefensible. Every successful drone delivery DoorDash facilitates proves that restaurants don't need a $6–$8 human driver or a 30% platform cut to reach customers.

The question isn't whether drone delivery will disrupt the incumbents. It's whether an $80 billion company can survive a technology that makes its core business model — paying humans to drive food around in cars — economically obsolete.
